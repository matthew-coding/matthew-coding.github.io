I was doing the JavaScript Olympics Repl.it exercise earlier today and I got totally stumped on the very last task it asked of me, to make a function that takes an assortment of values, an athletes name, the sport they play, their age, and a quote of theirs, and save it as an object so it could be referenced later.

I tried a lot of things and nothing seemed to work, I was doing all these for loops trying to cycle through every value, I used pseudocode to work out what steps I should take, I talked my poor dogs ear off going over what I was doing, nothing worked. I actually decided to try look at the sample solution they gave us. I usually try not to do this, but in this case I was clearly missing something, and I wanted to be able to work backwards, look at how to solve it, and correctly identify why this code worked but mine didn't.

It turned out the solution was exceptionally simple, all I had to do essentially was build an object and put the names of the values into it. I felt like a huge idiot, but I think I learned that bigger isn't always better. Just because I have a hammer doesn't mean every problem I come across needs to look like a nail. It was a good experience to have I think, just being able to see that sometimes what I need to do can be as simple as it seems

Now when I solved a problem in an elegant way, is a bit harder to talk about since most of my methods I tend to use are quite brutish. I am quite proud of my solution to the FizzBuzz Repl.it challenge, often for a lot of these coding challenges I struggle quite a lot, and this one honestly was no exception.

However this one sticks out for me because I successfully managed to use the pseudocode problem solving method effectively. I wrote down the steps I needed to take and successfully executed them after a bit of thought on how to tackle it. I even figured out the proper ordering of my commands after a bit of trial and error interpreting the error messages that popped up. It definitely taught me a few things about JavaScript, particularly in how JavaScript reads what you've written and in what order it does things.

I think out of the problem solving techniques we were introduced to in this sprint, the ones I'm most comfortable with using are

  • Pseudocode
  • Trying Stuff
  • Reading Error Messages
  • Googling
  • Asking Coaches for help

Pseudocode is just an absolute life saver, its super helpful to be able to just write down what I think I need to do. While sometimes the thing I think I need to do turns out to be wrong, it does give me a good starting point for coding.

Trying stuff is where I feel the most comfortable, I think I'm a very "hands on" type of learner, I absorb knowledge a lot faster when I can just try something, whether I succeed or fail.

Reading error messages has become something I've gotten quite accustomed to, and the initial sting of failure whenever the dreaded red text pops up has subsided quite a lot to the point where I see it as more of a helpful suggestion on JavaScripts part.

Googling problems occurs quite often for me, and the skill has gotten a bit easier since I've gotten more familiar with terms that JavaScript uses.

When all else fails as well I do know a good amount of people who know what they're doing in regards to coding and are always happy to give me a hand when I'm struggling with an issue. I do my best to not get an answer to the challenge im doing, but rather input on my method, or an explanation of why what I tried doesn’t work.

As for the other problem solving techniques presented to us, I think the rubber ducky method is still a work in progress for me. I don’t really have an inanimate object around that I feel comfortable talking to, so usually I just use my flatmates dog who is often napping on the couch nearby. He is a fantastic listener, and I think its still good to try to organize my thoughts a lot more
